Part 1

Reading the loop

Physiology
At a glance
  • x-axis = LV volume, y-axis = LV pressure. The loop runs counter-clockwise.
  • Each corner is a valve event; each side is a phase of the cardiac cycle.
  • Loop width = stroke volume; loop area = stroke work.
Physiology

The loop, corner by corner

Start at the bottom-right — the ventricle is full (end-diastolic volume, EDV). Go counter-clockwise: the four straight-ish sides are filling, isovolumetric contraction, ejection, and isovolumetric relaxation. The two bounding lines are the ESPVR (contractility) and the EDPVR (compliance).

High-yield

SV = EDV − ESV. Anything that widens the loop raises stroke volume; anything that shifts the right edge (EDV) or the left edge (ESV) changes it. Ejection fraction = SV / EDV.

Part 2

The four phases

Physiology
Physiology

Walking the cycle

  • ① → ② Isovolumetric contraction — both valves shut, volume fixed, pressure shoots up. Begins at S1 (mitral closure).
  • ② → ③ Ejection — aortic valve open; volume falls from EDV to ESV as blood leaves.
  • ③ → ④ Isovolumetric relaxation — both valves shut again, volume fixed, pressure falls. Begins at S2 (aortic closure).
  • ④ → ① Filling — mitral valve open; volume climbs back to EDV along the EDPVR.
Clinical correlation

The heart sounds bookend the isovolumetric phases: S1 is mitral/tricuspid closure (start of contraction), S2 is aortic/pulmonic closure (start of relaxation). That's why the loop's two vertical sides map exactly onto S1 and S2.

Isovolumetric = both valves closed Vertical side = constant volume Horizontal side = valve open
Part 3

Preload, afterload, contractility

Physiology
Physiology

Three levers, three deformations

Each variable moves a different edge of the loop. Learn the edge each one touches and you can predict the picture without memorizing it.

ChangeWhat movesEffect on the loop
↑ PreloadRight edge (EDV)Loop widens to the right → ↑ SV (Frank–Starling)
↑ AfterloadTop / ② heightAortic valve opens at higher pressure → ESV rises, loop narrows → ↓ SV
↑ ContractilityLeft edge (ESV) via ESPVRSteeper ESPVR → ventricle empties more → ↓ ESV, ↑ SV
Don’t confuse

Preload sets the starting (right) edge of the loop; afterload is the wall the ventricle pushes against during ejection (the height of ②). Raising afterload raises ESV — the ventricle can't empty as far — which is the opposite of raising contractility.

Part 4

Valve disease loops

PathologyPhysiology
Pathology

How each lesion deforms the picture

Valve disease breaks the clean rectangle in a characteristic way. The trick: ask whether the lesion corrupts a filling step or an ejection step, and whether a normally-closed valve is now leaking.

  • Aortic stenosis — ejection against a tight valve: very high systolic pressure (loop taller), reduced SV (loop narrower).
  • Aortic regurgitation — no true isovolumetric relaxation (aortic valve leaks): the loop loses its left vertical side and shifts right with a large SV.
  • Mitral stenosis — impaired filling: loop is small and shifted left; low EDV.
  • Mitral regurgitation — no true isovolumetric contraction (mitral leaks back): the loop loses its right vertical side; volume falls even before the aortic valve opens.
High-yield

A regurgitant valve erases the isovolumetric side next to it — that valve can't stay shut, so volume changes during a phase that should be constant-volume. AR kills isovolumetric relaxation; MR kills isovolumetric contraction.
