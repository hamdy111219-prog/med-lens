Topic 1

Cardiac anatomy & circulation

Anatomy Physiology
At a glance
  • Blood flows body → RA → RV → lungs → LA → LV → body, with a valve guarding every exit.
  • The left coronary artery supplies the anterior wall and most of the left ventricle; the RCA supplies the inferior wall and (usually) the SA & AV nodes.
  • Two AV valves (tricuspid, mitral) and two semilunar valves (pulmonic, aortic).
Anatomy

The four chambers & the path of blood

The heart is two pumps in series. The right heart handles deoxygenated blood at low pressure; the left heart drives oxygenated blood into the systemic circulation at high pressure — which is why the left ventricle wall is roughly three times thicker than the right.

Clinical correlation

Listen where the blood is heading, not where the valve sits: the aortic valve is loudest at the right 2nd intercostal space, pulmonic at the left 2nd, mitral at the apex.

Anatomy

Coronary supply

  • LAD (left anterior descending) — anterior wall + anterior ⅔ of septum. The most commonly occluded vessel.
  • LCX (left circumflex) — lateral wall of the LV.
  • RCA (right coronary) — inferior wall; supplies the SA and AV nodes in ~90% of people (right-dominant).
Don't confuse

The coronaries fill during diastole, not systole — tachycardia shortens diastole and can starve the myocardium even when cardiac output looks fine.

Topic 2

Cardiac action potentials & conduction

Physiology Pharmacology
At a glance
  • Ventricular myocytes have 5 phases (0–4); the plateau (phase 2) is the signature of cardiac muscle.
  • Pacemaker (SA/AV node) cells have no fast Na⁺ channels — their upstroke is driven by Ca²⁺, and phase 4 slow depolarization sets the heart rate.
  • Conduction order: SA node → AV node → bundle of His → Purkinje fibers.
Physiology

The ventricular action potential

Each phase is one ion telling a story. The plateau — inward Ca²⁺ balancing outward K⁺ — is what gives cardiac muscle its long refractory period and prevents tetany.

  • Phase 0 — rapid upstroke; voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open.
  • Phase 1 — brief dip; Na⁺ channels close, transient K⁺ efflux.
  • Phase 2 — plateau; voltage-gated Ca²⁺ influx balances K⁺ efflux.
  • Phase 3 — repolarization; Ca²⁺ channels close, K⁺ efflux dominates.
  • Phase 4 — resting potential maintained by K⁺ leak.
Physiology

Pacemaker cells are different

SA and AV nodal cells lack fast Na⁺ channels. Their unstable phase 4 (the funny current, If) slowly drifts toward threshold, then a Ca²⁺-driven upstroke fires. This automaticity is why the SA node sets the pace at 60–100 bpm.

Mnemonic

"Pacemakers Pick Calcium." Nodal upstroke = Ca²⁺ (slow), ventricular upstroke = Na⁺ (fast).

Pharmacology

Where antiarrhythmics act

The Vaughan-Williams classes map directly onto these phases — knowing the curve tells you the drug.

  • Class I (Na⁺ blockers) — slow phase 0 upstroke.
  • Class II (β-blockers) — slow nodal phase 4, reducing rate.
  • Class III (K⁺ blockers, e.g. amiodarone) — prolong phase 3 & the QT interval.
  • Class IV (Ca²⁺ blockers) — slow AV node conduction.
High-yield

Class III drugs prolong the QT → risk of torsades de pointes. This link between phase 3, QT, and torsades is tested constantly.

Topic 3

Cardiac output & the Frank–Starling relationship

Physiology
Physiology

The equation that runs the circulation

Cardiac output = heart rate × stroke volume. Stroke volume itself depends on three levers: preload, afterload, and contractility.

CO = HR × SV SV = EDV − ESV EF = SV / EDV MAP = CO × TPR
Physiology

Frank–Starling: stretch makes strength

Within limits, the more the ventricle fills (preload), the harder it contracts — stretched sarcomeres develop more force. Changing contractility shifts the entire curve up or down.

  • ↑ Contractility — catecholamines, digoxin. Curve shifts up.
  • ↓ Contractility — heart failure, β-blockers, ischemia, acidosis. Curve shifts down.
  • ↑ Preload — exercise, fluids, pregnancy. Move right along the curve.
Clinical correlation

A failing heart sits on a low, flat curve — it can't recruit more stroke volume from extra filling, so giving too much fluid just raises pressures and causes pulmonary edema.

Topic 4

Heart failure

Pathology Physiology Pharmacology
At a glance
  • Left HF backs up into the lungs → dyspnea, orthopnea, crackles.
  • Right HF backs up into the body → JVD, peripheral edema, hepatomegaly. Its #1 cause is left HF.
  • Drugs that improve mortality: ACE inhibitors / ARBs, β-blockers, aldosterone antagonists, SGLT2 inhibitors.
Pathology

Systolic vs diastolic — a pump problem vs a filling problem

Systolic (HFrEF)Diastolic (HFpEF)
Core defectWeak contractionStiff, non-compliant ventricle
Ejection fractionReduced (<40%)Preserved (≥50%)
VentricleDilated, thinHypertrophied, small cavity
Typical causePrior MI, dilated cardiomyopathyChronic hypertension, aging
Physiology

Why the body makes it worse

Falling output triggers RAAS and sympathetic activation — a sensible short-term fix that, sustained, raises afterload and preload until the failing ventricle is overwhelmed. This maladaptive remodeling is the target of nearly every effective HF drug.

↑ BNP ↑ RAAS ↑ Sympathetic tone Ventricular remodeling
Pharmacology

Treat the physiology, not just the symptoms

  • ACE-i / ARB — block RAAS; reduce afterload & remodeling. Improve survival.
  • β-blockers — blunt sympathetic overdrive. Improve survival (start low, go slow).
  • Aldosterone antagonists — spironolactone; anti-remodeling. Improve survival.
  • Loop diuretics — relieve congestion & edema (symptoms, not mortality).
Don't confuse

Diuretics make patients feel dramatically better but don't extend life. Step 1 loves the distinction between symptom relief and mortality benefit.

Topic 5

Congenital heart disease

Embryology Anatomy Pathology
Embryology

How the defect is built

The heart begins as a single tube that loops and septates around weeks 3–8. Failure to fully partition the chambers or great vessels produces a shunt — the central concept of congenital heart disease.

Mnemonic

"The 5 T's" of cyanosis: Truncus arteriosus, Transposition, Tricuspid atresia, Tetralogy of Fallot, Total anomalous pulmonary venous return.

Pathology

Left-to-right vs right-to-left shunts

  • Left → right (acyanotic): VSD, ASD, PDA. Higher left-sided pressure pushes oxygenated blood back to the lungs. Late cyanosis if untreated.
  • Right → left (cyanotic): the 5 T's. Deoxygenated blood bypasses the lungs → early cyanosis.
Don't confuse

An untreated left-to-right shunt can reverse once pulmonary pressures climb — Eisenmenger syndrome — turning a "pink" baby blue years later.

Anatomy

Tetralogy of Fallot — the four pieces

  • Pulmonary stenosis (the driver of severity)
  • Right ventricular hypertrophy
  • Overriding aorta
  • VSD
High-yield

Mnemonic "PROVe". The classic boot-shaped heart on X-ray, and the child who squats during "tet spells" to raise systemic resistance and reduce the right-to-left shunt.
